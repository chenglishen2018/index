Emulation Target Trial Study Plan: Comparing Treatments A and B for Lung Cancer

This document outlines a study plan for an emulation target trial designed to compare the effectiveness of two treatments, Treatment A and Treatment B, for lung cancer patients using observational data.

1. Introduction and Rationale

Background: Lung cancer remains a leading cause of cancer-related mortality worldwide. While randomized controlled trials (RCTs) are the gold standard for evaluating treatment efficacy, they often face limitations in generalizability, patient recruitment, and long-term follow-up. Observational data, such as electronic health records (EHRs) and administrative claims databases, offer a rich source of real-world evidence.

Rationale for Target Trial Emulation: Target trial emulation provides a robust framework to leverage observational data to mimic the design and analysis of a hypothetical RCT. This approach helps to minimize common biases in observational studies by explicitly defining the components of an ideal trial, including eligibility criteria, treatment assignment, follow-up, and outcome assessment.

Objective: To compare the effectiveness of Treatment A versus Treatment B on overall survival and disease progression-free survival in patients with lung cancer, using a target trial emulation approach on observational data.

2. Components of the Target Trial

The following components define the hypothetical target trial that this observational study will emulate:

2.1. Eligibility Criteria

  • Population: Patients diagnosed with primary lung cancer (specify stage, e.g., Stage III/IV non-small cell lung cancer, if applicable, based on data availability).
  • Age: $\ge 18$ years.
  • Diagnosis Date: Patients diagnosed within a specified time window (e.g., January 1, 2010 - December 31, 2023) to ensure data completeness and relevance.
  • Treatment Naivety: Patients must be treatment-na√Øve for lung cancer at the time of eligibility (i.e., no prior systemic therapy for lung cancer).
  • Availability of Data: Sufficient baseline clinical data (e.g., demographics, comorbidities, cancer stage, performance status, laboratory values) and follow-up data (treatment details, outcomes) must be available.
  • Exclusion Criteria:
    • Patients with a history of other active malignancies (excluding adequately treated non-melanoma skin cancer or carcinoma in situ).
    • Patients with insufficient follow-up data to ascertain treatment initiation or outcomes.
    • Patients receiving palliative care as the sole initial management strategy.

2.2. Treatment Strategies

  • Treatment A: Defined as initiation of Treatment A within a specified window (e.g., 30 days) after meeting eligibility criteria.
  • Treatment B: Defined as initiation of Treatment B within a specified window (e.g., 30 days) after meeting eligibility criteria.
  • Comparator: Patients who initiate Treatment A will be compared to those who initiate Treatment B.
  • "Intention-to-Treat" Principle: Patients will be assigned to their initial treatment strategy group based on the first treatment received after meeting eligibility, regardless of subsequent treatment modifications or discontinuations.

2.3. Time Zero (Trial Entry)

  • Definition: Time zero for each patient will be defined as the date of initiation of either Treatment A or Treatment B, provided they meet all eligibility criteria at or prior to this date. This is the "start of follow-up" for the target trial.

2.4. Outcomes

  • Primary Outcome:
    • Overall Survival (OS): Time from time zero until death from any cause. Censored at the end of study follow-up or last known contact.
  • Secondary Outcomes:
    • Disease Progression-Free Survival (PFS): Time from time zero until documented disease progression (based on clinical, radiological, or pathological evidence) or death from any cause, whichever occurs first. Censored at the end of study follow-up or last known contact. (Requires detailed progression data, which may be challenging in observational datasets).
    • Treatment-Related Adverse Events: Incidence of severe (Grade 3+) adverse events attributed to treatment (if reliably captured in the data).

2.5. Follow-up

  • Duration: Patients will be followed from time zero until the primary outcome event, end of study follow-up (e.g., December 31, 2024), or loss to follow-up, whichever comes first.
  • Censoring: Patients will be censored at the date of last contact or end of study follow-up if the outcome event has not occurred.

3. Data Sources and Extraction

  • Data Source: Specify the observational database(s) to be used (e.g., national cancer registries linked to administrative claims, large integrated healthcare system EHRs, specific hospital databases).
  • Data Elements:
    • Patient demographics (age, sex, race/ethnicity).
    • Diagnosis codes (ICD-9/10 for lung cancer, comorbidities).
    • Treatment codes (procedure codes, drug codes for Treatment A and B).
    • Date of diagnosis, treatment initiation, progression, and death.
    • Baseline clinical characteristics (e.g., ECOG performance status, tumor histology, stage, molecular markers if available).
    • Healthcare utilization data (hospitalizations, outpatient visits).

4. Statistical Analysis Plan

4.1. Causal Contrast and Exchangeability

  • The goal is to estimate the average treatment effect of Treatment A versus Treatment B.
  • To achieve exchangeability (i.e., that the groups are comparable as if randomized), confounding will be addressed.

4.2. Handling Time-Varying Confounders

  • Cloned Censoring and Weighting (Marginal Structural Models - MSM): This is the preferred method for handling time-varying confounders affected by prior treatment.
    • Step 1: Cloning: Create a "cloned" dataset where each patient is represented multiple times, once for each treatment strategy they could have initiated at time zero.
    • Step 2: Censoring: Apply censoring rules to mimic the "no switching" assumption of an RCT. Patients who switch treatments will be censored from their initial treatment group at the time of switching.
    • Step 3: Inverse Probability of Treatment Weighting (IPTW): Calculate weights to balance baseline and time-varying confounders between the treatment groups.
      • Weights will be estimated using logistic regression models for the probability of receiving Treatment A (or B) at time zero, conditional on baseline confounders, and for the probability of remaining on the initial treatment strategy, conditional on baseline and time-varying confounders.
      • Stabilized weights will be used to reduce variability.
    • Step 4: Outcome Model: A weighted Cox proportional hazards model will be used to estimate the hazard ratio for OS and PFS, with the treatment indicator as the primary exposure, accounting for the IPTW.
  • G-computation (Alternative/Sensitivity Analysis): If MSMs are not feasible or for sensitivity analysis, G-computation could be used. This involves fitting models for treatment assignment and outcome, then standardizing the outcome across treatment groups.

4.3. Baseline Confounder Adjustment

  • For baseline confounders not addressed by IPTW (or if IPTW is not used), standard regression adjustment (e.g., Cox regression) will be employed.
  • Potential baseline confounders include: age, sex, race/ethnicity, comorbidities (e.g., Charlson Comorbidity Index), cancer stage, histology, performance status, and prior treatments for other conditions.

4.4. Sensitivity Analyses

  • Unmeasured Confounding: Conduct sensitivity analyses to assess the potential impact of unmeasured confounding (e.g., using E-values).
  • Misclassification: Evaluate the impact of potential misclassification of outcomes or exposures.
  • Varying Time Windows: Test the robustness of results to different definitions of the treatment initiation window.
  • Alternative Analytic Methods: Compare results from MSMs with other methods like propensity score matching or stratification, if appropriate, for baseline confounding.

4.5. Statistical Software

  • Statistical analyses will be performed using appropriate software (e.g., R, SAS, Python with `pandas`, `statsmodels`, `lifelines`).

5. Ethical Considerations

  • Institutional Review Board (IRB) Approval: The study protocol will be submitted to and approved by the relevant IRB.
  • Data Privacy: All patient data will be de-identified and handled in accordance with privacy regulations (e.g., HIPAA).
  • Transparency: The study design, methods, and results will be reported transparently.

6. Limitations

  • Data Availability and Quality: Reliance on observational data means that certain clinical variables (e.g., detailed performance status, specific molecular markers, precise progression dates) may be missing or inconsistently recorded.
  • Residual Confounding: Despite sophisticated methods, unmeasured confounding remains a possibility in observational studies.
  • Misclassification Bias: Errors in coding or data entry can lead to misclassification of exposures, outcomes, or confounders.
  • Generalizability: Findings are generalizable to the population reflected in the specific observational database used.
